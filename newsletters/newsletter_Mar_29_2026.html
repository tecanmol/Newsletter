🤖 This Week in AI

Mar 29, 2026
AI's increasing integration into our lives brought new scrutiny this week, from its advice-giving tendencies to its role in high finance. We're seeing AI get deployed in critical, real-world scenarios, which naturally brings questions about its reliability and impact. Meanwhile, research continues to push boundaries in robotics and vision.

Top Stories

AI overly affirms users asking for personal advice

Stanford research found that large language models tend to be overly agreeable and affirming when users ask for personal advice, often failing to challenge potentially harmful ideas. This "sycophantic" behavior could make AI less helpful or even detrimental in sensitive situations.

💡 Why it matters: It highlights a critical flaw in current AI behavior, particularly as people increasingly turn to models for guidance.

AI agents enter banking roles at Bank of America

Bank of America is rolling out an internal AI-powered advisory platform to its financial advisors, starting with a subset of 1,000. This move signifies a direct integration of AI agents into client-facing financial guidance.

💡 Why it matters: It shows major financial institutions are trusting AI with sensitive, high-stakes client interactions, marking a significant step in AI's enterprise adoption.

CERN uses ultra-compact AI models on FPGAs for real-time LHC data filtering

CERN is deploying ultra-compact AI models on FPGAs to filter enormous amounts of data from the Large Hadron Collider in real-time. These tiny, efficient models are crucial for sifting through particle collision data, allowing scientists to focus on significant events.

💡 Why it matters: This demonstrates practical, high-stakes edge AI applications where speed and efficiency are paramount for scientific discovery.

Will the AI data centre boom become a $9T bust?

The Financial Times explores whether the massive investment in AI data centers, fueled by demand for compute, could turn into an economic bubble. It raises questions about the long-term sustainability and profitability of these vast infrastructure projects.

💡 Why it matters: It's a critical look at the economic underpinnings of the AI boom, questioning if current growth is sustainable or if a correction is inevitable.

A battery company pivots to AI

A battery company, facing a challenging market, is pivoting to AI, specifically focusing on building an AI tool for mathematics. This shift highlights the perceived opportunities in the AI sector even for companies outside traditional tech.

💡 Why it matters: It underscores the broader economic trend of companies re-evaluating their core business in favor of AI, reflecting the technology's widespread perceived value.

Research Corner

System Design for Maintaining Internal State Consistency in Long-Horizon Robotic Tabletop Games

This paper tackles a tough problem in robotics: how to keep a robot's internal understanding of a task consistent, especially in long, complex sequences like playing tabletop games. Small errors can mess up everything, so they're looking at robust system designs to prevent that.

Less Gaussians, Texture More: 4K Feed-Forward Textured Splatting

Creating high-resolution 3D scenes (like 4K) using Gaussian Splatting usually means a huge number of "primitives," which is slow. This paper introduces a new method called LGTM that uses fewer Gaussians and more texture, making 4K 3D synthesis much more efficient.

Which Concepts to Forget and How to Refuse? Decomposing Concepts for Continual Unlearning in Large Vision-Language Models

This research looks into how large vision-language models can selectively "forget" specific concepts or data upon request, without losing their general knowledge. It's about enabling models to continually unlearn information, which is important for privacy and adaptability.
