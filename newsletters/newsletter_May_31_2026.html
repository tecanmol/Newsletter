🤖 This Week in AI

May 31, 2026

This week, the conversation around AI got a bit more nuanced, moving beyond just awe and into some surprising public sentiment.

We saw significant investment in AI infrastructure, but also strong signals that the societal impact of AI, from job anxiety to ethical frameworks, is becoming a much louder part of the discussion. It's clear that while the tech charges forward, the human response is catching up, sometimes with a boo.

OpenRouter Raises $113M Series B

OpenRouter, a platform that lets developers access multiple AI models through a single API, secured a hefty $113 million in Series B funding. This investment suggests strong confidence in the need for a unified gateway to the fragmented world of large language models.

💡 Why it matters: This funding solidifies OpenRouter's position as a key piece of infrastructure in the multi-model AI landscape, making it easier for developers to experiment and deploy.

AI Gets Booed at Graduation Ceremonies

MIT Technology Review reported on a surprising trend: AI references, like those from former Google CEO Eric Schmidt at the University of Arizona, were met with boos from graduating students. This indicates a growing skepticism or perhaps even resentment towards AI among those entering the workforce.

💡 Why it matters: This public reaction is a stark reminder that not everyone is embracing AI with open arms, and it highlights a potential disconnect between tech visionaries and the general public.

Pope Leo XIV Releases Encyclical on AI

Pope Leo XIV issued "Magnifica Humanitas" ("Magnificent Humanity"), a new encyclical on artificial intelligence, emphasizing that "Technology is never neutral." The document calls for individuals to act with courage and solidarity in shaping AI's future.

💡 Why it matters: This represents a major ethical and philosophical statement from a global leader, providing a template for human-centric engagement with AI's societal implications.

AI Job Grief: A Psychological Crisis

A new blog post highlighted "AI job grief," describing a psychological crisis affecting tech workers who feel their skills are becoming obsolete or their jobs are threatened by AI. This emotional toll is a significant, often overlooked, side effect of rapid AI advancement.

💡 Why it matters: Understanding and addressing the human psychological impact of AI on the workforce is crucial for a healthy transition and to avoid widespread distress.

AI Act Delays and Agent Traffic Surge

This week's Batch newsletter touched on two key developments: further delays in the implementation of the EU's AI Act, and a notable increase in online traffic being driven by AI agents. These trends show contrasting paces in policy and practical application.

💡 Why it matters: Policy is struggling to keep up with rapid AI deployment, while AI agents are already making a tangible impact on web activity, signaling a shift in how we interact online.

ESAM++: Efficient Online 3D Perception on the Edge

This paper introduces a new method for real-time 3D scene perception that works well on devices with limited computing power, like robots or AR/VR headsets. It's an improved version of EmbodiedSAM, designed to be fast and efficient for tasks where every millisecond counts.

BEAT: Rhythm-Elastic Alignment for Agentic Music-guided Movie Trailer Generation

Researchers developed an AI that can automatically create movie trailers by selecting film shots and syncing them with music. Unlike older methods, this one understands that professional editing has a flexible rhythm, making the generated trailers feel more natural and engaging.

BiasEdit: A Training-Free Bias-Detect-and-Edit Framework for Learning Fair Visual Classifiers

This paper presents a framework to detect and reduce biases in visual AI models, like those used for image recognition. It works without needing to retrain the entire model, making it a practical way to ensure AI systems are fair and don't learn harmful stereotypes from web data.
